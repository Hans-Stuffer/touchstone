# Background: the "model then verify" loop and where it comes from

touchstone rests on one claim about how reliable software gets written. You do not check a program for correctness after the fact by staring harder. You first find the structure the problem already has, an algebra or an invariant or a law, and then you hand that structure to a machine that can prove or refute it exactly. The skill calls this "find the structure first, then pick the engine that proves it." This document traces that idea through the literature, marks where the literature backs it strongly, and is honest about the places where it is thinner than the marketing around formal methods usually admits.

The whole thing is a loop with two halves. The first half is modeling: name the operation, ask which laws it obeys, match it to a known structure. The second half is verification: discharge the law with an exact engine, then say plainly what was actually proved. The repo's own framing, "translate then solve" and "generate then verify," is just these two halves named differently.

## The "find the algebra" half

The oldest and purest version of model-first is the program-calculation tradition, sometimes called Squiggol after its dense notation. Lambert Meertens, in "Algorithmics: Towards Programming as a Mathematical Activity" (in *Mathematics and Computer Science*, CWI Monographs Vol. 1, de Bakker, Hazewinkel and Lenstra, eds., North-Holland, 1986, pp. 289-334), set out the manifesto: start from a specification that is obviously correct even if hopelessly slow, then reach an efficient program by correctness-preserving algebraic steps. Correctness is not a separate proof obligation tacked on at the end. It is a byproduct of how the program was derived.

Richard Bird's "An Introduction to the Theory of Lists" (Oxford PRG Technical Monograph PRG-56, 1986; also in *Logic of Programming and Calculi of Discrete Design*, Broy, ed., NATO ASI Series F Vol. 36, Springer, 1987, pp. 3-42) made this concrete for lists: a point-free calculus of map, reduce and filter with algebraic laws like promotion and the homomorphism lemma, used to transform an obvious-but-slow list program into a fast one equationally. Erik Meijer, Maarten Fokkinga and Ross Paterson then generalized the move from lists to any datatype in "Functional Programming with Bananas, Lenses, Envelopes and Barbed Wire" (FPCA 1991, LNCS 523, Springer, pp. 124-144). The recursion schemes there, catamorphisms and anamorphisms and the rest, are not invented per problem. They fall out of the datatype's initial-algebra structure. The structure dictates the laws. The book-length synthesis is Richard Bird and Oege de Moor's *Algebra of Programming* (Prentice Hall, 1997), where whole design paradigms, greedy and dynamic-programming and divide-and-conquer, are derived and justified in a relational, categorical calculus.

Underneath all of this sits the initial-algebra view of datatypes from Joseph Goguen, James Thatcher, Eric Wagner and Jesse Wright (the ADJ group) in "Initial Algebra Semantics and Continuous Algebras" (*Journal of the ACM* 24(1):68-95, 1977). The slogan is that a type is its algebra. That single idea grounds both the categorical recursion schemes above and the algebraic specification of abstract data types, where the equations defining an ADT are the model. Maude (Manuel Clavel, Francisco Duran, Steven Eker, Patrick Lincoln, Narciso Marti-Oliet, Jose Meseguer and Jose Quesada, "Maude: specification and programming in rewriting logic," *Theoretical Computer Science* 285(2):187-243, 2002) made that lineage executable: equations define the algebra, rewriting runs it, and the same equations are the object you verify. Spec is model, model is checkable.

Monads are the same move arriving in mainstream functional programming. Philip Wadler's "Comprehending Monads" (LFP 1990, pp. 61-78; expanded in *Mathematical Structures in Computer Science* 2(4):461-493, 1992) and "Monads for Functional Programming" (in *Advanced Functional Programming*, Jeuring and Meijer, eds., LNCS 925, Springer, 1995, pp. 24-52) showed that state, exceptions and parsing are instances of one law-bearing structure. Recognizing the monad is the find-the-structure step. The three monad laws are what you then rely on, and can prove, when you refactor.

This is exactly touchstone's modeling table, where merging wants to be a semilattice, folding wants to be a monoid, precedence wants to be a lattice, and dedup wants to be an equivalence relation with a normal form. The mapping from problem shape to algebra to laws-to-prove is the part of the skill that does the most work, and it is well-grounded.

There is a sharper, cheaper version of the same idea that needs no calculus. Encode the invariant directly in the type so that illegal states cannot be constructed. Yaron Minsky's "make illegal states unrepresentable" ("Effective ML Revisited," Jane Street Tech Blog, March 9, 2011) collapses "find the structure" and "prove the law" into a single act the compiler enforces for free. Choose a sum type that admits only the legal cases, and the bad state fails to compile rather than failing at runtime. This is the touchstone "state machine to impossible states you cannot even represent" row, and it is the lowest-cost, highest-adoption form of the whole discipline.

One honest caveat the literature itself raises. This tradition assumes the hard part is already done, that someone has spotted the monoid or the functor or the initial algebra. *Algebra of Programming* is famously demanding precisely because the creative, non-mechanical work is in discovering the right algebra. Once the structure is named, proving the law is comparatively routine. So "structure first, law second" is correct, but the skill in real engineering lives disproportionately in the structure-finding step, which no engine does for you.

## The "verify" half

The verification side has its own clean lineage. C.A.R. Hoare's "An Axiomatic Basis for Computer Programming" (*Communications of the ACM* 12(10):576-580, 1969) is the founding move: a precondition-postcondition triple turns a program into a logical claim you can prove rather than merely run. Edsger Dijkstra's *A Discipline of Programming* (Prentice-Hall, 1976) inverts it, using weakest-precondition predicate transformers so the postcondition derives the code. Bertrand Meyer's "Applying Design by Contract" (*IEEE Computer* 25(10):40-51, 1992) brought those obligations down to working engineers as preconditions, postconditions and invariants embedded in real code and checked against it. touchstone's discount example, with its leading asserts as preconditions and a trailing assert as the invariant, is design-by-contract in miniature.

The structure-first idea shows up on the verification side as lightweight formal methods. Daniel Jackson coined the phrase in *Software Abstractions: Logic, Language, and Analysis* (Revised Edition, MIT Press, 2011; first edition 2006): model an abstraction in a tiny relational logic (Alloy), then let an automated analyzer hunt for a counterexample instead of constructing a proof by hand. Leslie Lamport's *Specifying Systems: The TLA+ Language and Tools for Hardware and Software Engineers* (Addison-Wesley, 2002) is the industrial form: specify the system in temporal logic before building it, then model-check the spec for invariant and liveness violations. Hillel Wayne's *Practical TLA+: Planning Driven Development* (Apress, 2018) is the working-engineer on-ramp to the same method. The common thread with touchstone is that you model in a small mathematical notation and let an automated procedure break it, rather than hand-proving anything.

The engines touchstone actually routes to each instantiate the verify step, and each has a real provenance. Z3 (Leonardo de Moura and Nikolaj Bjorner, "Z3: An Efficient SMT Solver," TACAS 2008, LNCS 4963, Springer, pp. 337-340) is the decision procedure that discharges first-order constraints over arithmetic, bit-vectors, arrays and uninterpreted functions. The repo's "assert the negation, check for UNSAT" recipe is the standard way to use it to prove a universal claim. Dafny (K. Rustan M. Leino, "Dafny: An Automatic Program Verifier for Functional Correctness," LPAR-16 2010, LNCS 6355, Springer, pp. 348-370) shows the full arc the touchstone verify step mirrors: write the invariant, lower it to an intermediate language, prove it with an SMT solver.

The testing engines occupy the complementary niche of refutation. James King's "Symbolic Execution and Program Testing" (*Communications of the ACM* 19(7):385-394, 1976) introduced running code over symbolic inputs and solving the accumulated path constraints, the technique CrossHair is built on. Koen Claessen and John Hughes's "QuickCheck: A Lightweight Tool for Random Testing of Haskell Programs" (ICFP 2000, pp. 268-279) founded property-based testing: state a universally quantified law, then search random inputs for a counterexample. David MacIver and Zac Hatfield-Dodds's "Hypothesis: A new approach to property-based testing" (*Journal of Open Source Software* 4(43):1891, 2019) is the mainstream Python descendant, with automatic shrinking to a minimal failing case, and is the engine a Python codebase like touchstone would actually invoke.

The distinction between these two families is the most important caveat in the whole repo, and the repo gets it right. Z3, Dafny and abstract interpretation prove: they certify a property over all inputs. Property-based testing and time-budgeted symbolic execution refute: they find counterexamples but, unbounded, do not certify universal correctness. King's own method is only complete for loop-free or bounded paths. This is exactly why touchstone insists that Z3 returning UNSAT is a proof while CrossHair finding no counterexample is not, and why "find the structure first" is load-bearing rather than cosmetic. When the structure is a semilattice or a monoid or a decidable theory, the law is provable. Absent that structure, you are testing, not proving.

Two structures deserve their own mention because they are the cleanest cases of the thesis. Conflict-free replicated data types (Marc Shapiro, Nuno Preguica, Carlos Baquero and Marek Zawirski, "Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types," SSS 2011, LNCS 6976, Springer, pp. 386-400) make convergence fall out for free the moment you recognize a join-semilattice with a commutative, associative, idempotent merge. You do not engineer convergence case by case. You spot the algebra and the law follows. This is the touchstone "merging wants to be a semilattice" row almost verbatim. Abstract interpretation (Patrick Cousot and Radhia Cousot, "Abstract Interpretation: A Unified Lattice Model for Static Analysis of Programs by Construction or Approximation of Fixpoints," POPL 1977, pp. 238-252) supplies the lattice, Galois-connection and fixpoint theory that makes "one abstract proof covers all concrete runs" rigorous, the formal backbone of sound static analysis.

## Industrial evidence that it pays off

The model-then-verify discipline is not just academic. It has shipped. Chris Newcombe, Tim Rath, Fan Zhang, Bogdan Munteanu, Marc Brooker and Michael Deardeuff's "How Amazon Web Services Uses Formal Methods" (*Communications of the ACM* 58(4):66-73, 2015) reported TLA+ specs of ten production systems that caught design bugs testing had missed, including a bug in DynamoDB, two in S3, and an EBS bug that required a thirty-five-step trace to trigger. The same paper notes a bug found even in a proposed fix. That is the design tier of the loop paying off at hyperscale. Zelkova, described in John Backes and colleagues' "Semantic-based Automated Reasoning for AWS Access Policies using SMT" (FMCAD 2018, pp. 1-9), reframes IAM authorization as a decidable "less-or-equally-permissive than" relation encoded into SMT and checked constantly in production. Modeling plus a decision procedure, run at industrial scale.

Code-level proof of shipping systems is also real, though it is heavier. seL4 (Gerwin Klein et al., "seL4: Formal Verification of an OS Kernel," SOSP 2009, pp. 207-220) is a machine-checked functional-correctness proof of a general-purpose microkernel, by refinement from an abstract spec down to C in Isabelle/HOL. CompCert (Xavier Leroy, "Formal Verification of a Realistic Compiler," *Communications of the ACM* 52(7):107-115, 2009) is a Coq-verified optimizing C compiler with a machine-checked semantic-preservation proof, mature enough for avionics, protecting the trust chain that a buggy compiler would otherwise void. Amazon's s2n TLS library (Andrey Chudnov et al., "Continuous Formal Verification of Amazon s2n," CAV 2018, LNCS 10982, Springer, pp. 430-446) proves C code equivalent to a Cryptol spec and re-runs the proofs in CI on every push, treating verification as a continuously maintained invariant rather than a one-shot stunt.

Ben Moseley and Peter Marks's "Out of the Tar Pit" (Software Practice Advancement, 2006) supplies the why under all of this. Complexity, chiefly mutable state, is what makes systems unanalyzable, so separating logic from state and control is the precondition for any reasoning at all. Find the essential structure before you build. That is the modeling half stated as a thesis about complexity.

## The modern LLM-and-verifier framing

touchstone's actual configuration, an LLM that proposes and an exact engine that checks, is the current research frontier. Yuhuai Wu and colleagues' "Autoformalization with Large Language Models" (NeurIPS 2022) showed that an LLM can translate informal math into a formal Isabelle spec, making "state the law formally" a machine task. Albert Jiang and colleagues' "Draft, Sketch, and Prove: Guiding Formal Theorem Provers with Informal Proofs" (ICLR 2023) operationalized the loop directly: the model drafts an informal proof, sketches a formal skeleton, and an exact prover discharges the subgoals. DeepSeek-Prover-V2 (Z.Z. Ren et al., arXiv:2504.21801, 2025) pushes this with reinforcement learning where a Lean checker grades every step.

In software specifically, the verifier-gated pattern is the same as touchstone's generate-then-verify. Md Rakib Hossain Misu, Cristina Lopes, Iris Ma and James Noble's "Towards AI-Assisted Synthesis of Verified Dafny Methods" (*Proc. ACM Softw. Eng.* 1, FSE 2024) has an LLM produce Dafny methods that must pass the Dafny verifier before acceptance. Clover (Chuyue Sun, Ying Sheng, Oded Padon and Clark Barrett, "Clover: Closed-Loop Verifiable Code Generation," in *AI Verification*, SAIV 2024, LNCS 14846, Springer, pp. 134-155) reduces correctness to a consistency check among code, docstrings and formal annotations via Dafny plus an LLM. The reason this works is the asymmetry Bradley Brown and colleagues identify in "Large Language Monkeys: Scaling Inference Compute with Repeated Sampling" (arXiv:2407.21787, 2024): when an automatic verifier exists, repeated sampling turns cheap coverage into solved problems; when it does not, selection plateaus. The engine, not the model, is the source of truth. That is precisely why touchstone wires exact engines in rather than trusting the model's self-assessment.

## The limits, stated honestly

Three caveats run through the literature and the repo should be read against them.

First, the spec is the hard part, not the proof. Hillel Wayne's "Why Don't People Use Formal Methods?" (hillelwayne.com, January 21, 2019) argues that for most engineers the high-value step is design specification, while full code verification is "obnoxiously hard" and rarely clears its cost bar. Finding the right spec is itself one of the biggest challenges. This matches the program-calculation caveat above: the creative work is naming the structure, and no tool automates it. It also matches what the vericoding work finds. Sergiu Bursuc and colleagues' "A benchmark for vericoding: formally verified program synthesis" (arXiv:2509.22908, 2025; accepted to the Dafny 2026 workshop at POPL 2026) reports that natural-language descriptions barely improve results once a formal spec exists. The spec carries the weight, and authoring correct requires and ensures clauses is still expert-scarce.

Second, adoption is mostly a cultural barrier, not a tooling one. Wayne's central observation is that model checkers already removed the hard proof labor, yet engineers do not adopt them because the spec feels disconnected from running code. A tool that lowers the activation energy, which is the layer touchstone tries to occupy, is fighting a habit problem more than a capability problem.

Third, the scope is genuinely narrow, and the repo says so. "Out of the Tar Pit" and the AWS experience both locate the payoff in a small, high-stakes slice: invariants over money, auth, data corruption and state. Most code is glue with no clean spec, and forcing a prover onto it manufactures false confidence. touchstone's own "honest scope" of five to fifteen percent of a repo is consistent with where the literature says proof pays. Push verification all the way down to code and you are in seL4 and CompCert territory, justified only for safety-, security- or trust-chain-critical cores.

One technical limit the repo under-states relative to the literature. Decision procedures are not always decisive. Z3 can return "unknown" on undecidable or hard fragments such as nonlinear integer arithmetic or unbounded quantifiers, and a constraint solver can hit its time limit. "Unknown" is neither proof nor disproof, the same status the repo correctly assigns to CrossHair finding nothing. And one phrase to avoid, "walks every path," overstates what symbolic execution does. The literature since King 1976 is unambiguous that path explosion makes exhaustive exploration infeasible in practice, which is exactly why no counterexample is not a proof. Symbolic execution searches paths within a time budget; it does not exhaust them.

The net is that touchstone's thesis is well-supported. Find the structure, then prove the law with an exact engine, is endorsed across program calculation, lightweight formal methods, the industrial record and the modern LLM-plus-verifier work. The honest edges are that the structure-finding and spec-writing are the scarce skills the engines do not supply, that the high-ROI tier is design-level modeling rather than whole-program proof, and that "proof" only applies when the structure is a decidable one.
